UX Case Study

Vanguard Year in Review

Working with Vanguard to transform the Vanguard Year in Review into a more encompassing and beginner friendly financial platform, that remains accessible to revisit and understand.

UX Research UI Design Prototyping
View Case Study
01

Project Overview

The Challenge

Navigation is limited to a singular desired flow. Some users just want to see the numbers. Some just want to know if they’re on track to retire. The original design has a clearly defined desired flow that limits a users ability to use the tool to fit their investing style. With a customizable platform like the digital advisor, there are users who just want to see the numbers behind their summary. And there are some who want to keep it simple: are they are on track to retire.

The Audience

Age 50+
Average to low tech-literacy
In or nearing retirement

Participants

Participant 1

55 years old female, not retired yet, Sonographer at Regional Hospital.

  • Wants context, not just raw figures
  • Compares year-over-year performance

Participant 3

Basic Flow

58 year old male, not retired, works as an Interventional Radiologist.

  • Low financial literacy
  • Easily overwhelmed by too much data
  • Wants a clear yes/no on retirement readiness

Participant 4

Advanced Flow

56 year old female, non retired, Data Analyst at Retirement Company.

  • Wants specific numbers & percentages
  • Comfortable with financial jargon
  • Reads every line of their statements

Personas

The Simplicity Seeker

Basic Flow

A working professional who doesn't have the time or desire to learn financial jargon. They check their account occasionally and want a friendly, digestible summary that reassures them without requiring deep analysis.

  • Advisor-dependent, checks in periodically
  • Prefers plain-language explanations
  • Needs simple, clear insights

The Analytical Expert

Advanced Flow

A semi-retired professional who tracks market trends. Very serious about his retirement plan. Since his early 20s, he has focused on staying ahead in savings and investment. He appreciates charts but wants the Year in Review to contextualize data.

  • Wants detailed numerical breakdowns
  • Financially educated & data-driven
  • Comfortable interpreting charts & metrics

The Anxious Retiree

Basic Flow

Recently retired and new to reviewing investment summaries on their own. They feel overwhelmed by financial terminology and just want to know: am I on track? Simplicity is everything.

  • Easily overwhelmed by too much data
  • Wants a clear yes/no on readiness
  • Avoids complex tables

The Hands-on Planner

Advanced Flow

Nearing retirement and actively managing their portfolio transition. They want granular, numerical data — returns by asset class, benchmark comparisons, and fee breakdowns.

  • Compares year-over-year performance
  • Wants context for market shifts
  • Analyzes asset allocation

What makes this design unique is the identification of these two distinct user types — rather than forcing everyone through the same flow, we tailored the experience to match each group’s comfort level and goals.

The Goal

Our flows are designed to give the user to freedom to decide how involved they want their Year In Review Experience to me. This preserves as much information as possible without overwhelming users who may not have the deepest understanding of finances or can give users all the information about their investments depending on the routes they pick.

02

Research & Insights

Focus on what mattered — not everything we did.

Usability Testing

Key Findings

Positive Findings: Users liked the font and color scheme of the UI and thought it was easy to read and consistent throughout webpage, specifically the homepage. The users enjoyed the “Next Steps” section after the Year in Review section Minor Findings: Some users felt that information was not easily accessible. We recommend having frequently referenced information remain the same between slides. The year in review had pages that users expressed were unnecessary. One point the user clicks “start review” only to be directed to another page where they have to click the same button. Some users thought the planning for 2026 section was too early especially since they were still scrolling through their 2025 review Major Problems: Multiple users expressed that they had major difficulties with scrolling through the prototype because there was no scroll bar. Two finger scrolling on a laptop did not seem so be intuitive for them, and this presented a repeated barrier during usability tests. Uneven info distribution with some slides were dense with information, while others were relatively empty. This prevented the year-in-review experience from flowing as smoothly as it could have

Audience Insight

What we included

We focused on how retirement savings would fit into the lives of our personas. What does their day-to-day look like, and what details are important to them. We wanted each persona to have a clearly defined personal relationship with investing.

Empathy / Persona

How we decided what mattered

We wanted to look at our participants Likert scale results and analyze patterns to make sure we could create cohesive personas. Based on those results we started creating persona notes for any outliers or very grouped results we saw. We also wanted to make sure we were able to encapsulate the main different types of users present.

Thought Process

After our disscussion with teamates we did the card sort and from there created two flows that could be a realistic possibility for our team given the research we had gathered about Vanguard, its target audience, and our usability test for our participants. We collected all participant information from our likert scale questions and quantified them to see where we can have areas of improvment

From our findings in the likert scale questions we started to see patterns in terms of what participants thought about Vanguard and the things we had observed in the usability tests. From this we began to draft our personas and organize our personas based on the information we had gathered from our Likert scales. After creating our personas we started to think about how to display the information we had gathered in a way that would be most beneficial for our user, so we came up with two distint user flows.

C

Key Decisions

Decision 1:

Our group decided to create our model based on flow 2. While flow 1 provided a less complect experience for our user, in terms of flow, flow 2 gave the user two pathways, the quick view and the detailed View This would allow the user to choose the level of detail they would like in order to view the information presented to them. The quick view is great for users who are not too knowledgable on financals or get overwhelmed easier. The detailed view is great for users who are more knowledgable or want more information.

Decision 2:

Removing generic images from the year-in-review design, agreeing they would not be missed and would allow for more information to be included.

Decision 3:

Changing the “Go to Checklist” option in the initial po-pup, because it doesn’t provide meaningful context—especially for first-time users. It drops them into a feature without explaining what the year in review is, why it matters, or how to navigate it. This can create confusion and reduce engagement early on. So, we decided to do a “Skip to Summary” option which is much more intentional. It clearly communicates where the user is going and sets expectations for what they’ll see. More importantly, it frames the year in review as something users can return to over time, rather than a one-time interaction.

Decision 4:

Making the new year in review revistable by creating an extensive summary of the year, which you can go to by clicking our new "Skip to Summary" page while making sure the year in review stays acessesable permenently instead of disapearing after a month of its launch in the new year.

07

Reflection

What I Learned

I learned a lot through this project especially in terms of working with a client, developing and pitching ideas, and gaining a deeper understanding of UX as a whole. One of the biggest takeaways for me was how collaborative the process is. Creating a project like the one we’re presenting requires continuous communication, iteration, and input from multiple perspectives. It’s a much longer and more involved process than it might initially seem, but that’s what ultimately leads to a more thoughtful and functional design that aligns with both the client’s goals and the needs of the target users. I also really enjoyed the creative aspect of the course. It pushed me to think more critically about how ideas evolve from initial concepts into fully developed experiences. Another insight I gained was about pitching. Before this class, I assumed pitches needed to be much longer and more detailed. However, I’ve learned that effective pitching is often about being clear, concise, and intentional, focusing on communicating the core idea in a compelling and efficient way

What I'd Improve

I would like to imrove on expanding this project. I think the year in review in general is a very big project and the clinent base is so diverse, that I think we could furthur improve on the quick view aspect to make it more descriptive and beginner friendly. I also think adding icons that when you hovor over to show certain financial jargon to better help the user would be very helpful. I think that even if the user is an experienced finanical person, its still very usuful to include helpful tools like that.

What Surprised Me

Going through this process I realized, I really enjoyed the collaborative process. With my teammates we were able to generate lots of ideas based off of our individuals ones and strengthened them. I was also surprised by the chalenges I had to face working with a team. A lot of the projects we had to do had to be heavily cordinated and everyone was busy so we had to work around a lot of peoples schedules

How This Shaped My Thinking

I learned how crucial UX research is to the process of UX design. When I first started this class I thought UX design mostly included creating Figma designs and making things look more aesthetically pleasing and structured. User research, I discovered, goes beyond basic demographics — it's really about understanding how people think and behave. This became clear when building our user personas, which ended up being shaped less by who the users were and more by how they engaged with financial tools, such as how frequently they checked their accounts or the way they approached financial decisions.
